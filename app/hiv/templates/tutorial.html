HIV is a virus that infects human immune cells.

An HIV infection lasts for many years.

The viral load is the concentration of virions in the blood.

CD4+ counts indicate the concentration of target cells.

You can browse this kind of data in the physiology section.

How can HIV survive despite the immune system?


HIV accumulates genetic mutations, and some allow it to escape the immune system.

However, many other mutations are seen in the HIV genome.

A new mutation arises in one virion, but might spread across the viral population as time passes.

See the sections on allele frequencies and phylogenies.

What regions of the HIV genome are mutating?

  • Diverse regions, such as the V loops, harbour many mutations.
  • Conserved regions, like the RRE, hardly tolerate any changes.
